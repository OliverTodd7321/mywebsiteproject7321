1.1 - Describe the purpose of IT in business.

IT, or Information Technology has become so ingrained in almost every aspect of our lives, it would be hard to imagine a world without it, no pun intended. We now live in the digital age. A time where an individual's mobile telepohonic device contains more computational power than the flight computers of the Saturn V - the rocket that launched the bravest of humanity on the voyages through the cold, dark, emptiness of space, to land humans upon our moon.
To describe the purpose of IT in business is a very complex task. So let us first clearly define IT. IT is defined as 'the study or use of systems (especially computers and telecommunications) for the storing, retrieving and sending of information'.
We use IT in the most ingenious ways, from something so simple as the sending of electronic mail, referred to as email, to the tracking of sales, expenses, productivity, customer servicing, the list is almost infinite. And I must stress the word almost there. IT is vitally important in how businesses store and manage their data in the modern world, from the smallest of bakeries to the largest corperations. Gone are the days of paper filing systems, now we have the ability to access that data globally and almost instantaneously.
Modern businesses may also use IT in order to tactically improve their customer service. From direct feedback, to website 'hits', the tracking of customer activity has helped many businesses, such as the company Amazon, go from online book retailer, to now being a multi-trillion USD logistics company.

1.4 - Describe the risks that might impact digital projects - what causes digital projects to fail? Why?

Risk is simply defined as not going according to the intiialized plan. Whether this be the project runnine over-time or over-budget. There are more prominent risks associated with digital projects, such a cyber attacks, or hacking.
An important factor in determining risk are the knowns and unknowns. These fall into 4 distinct catergories.
Known Knowns - These are problems or delays that you expect and allows for the planning of counter-measures to be implemented.
Known unknowns - These are problems that are known to be unsolvable or not known to be solvable - there is a slight difference.
Unknown Knowns - These are unexpected problems that arise that are easily solved with known solutions or fixes
Unknown Unknowns - These are unexpected problems that may not have a solvable solution.

1.5 - Describe how you would go about selecting and using IT systems and software – how do you select the right kind of technology for the job?

Choosing the correct technologies for a project can greatly increase productivity and efficiency. What must firstly be considered is the project/product requirements. It is important not to overload the project, using only as many technologies as utterly necessary. There is a middle ground that each project leader must find between using up-and-coming technologies, and the technologies that programmers and developers have knowleadge on.
The project must also be scalable and maintainable. Questions such as 'Can the program handle the intended level of user traffic?' 'Is it cross-platform?' or 'Will the program be able to handle future developments or new additions?' must be considered. Maintenance must also be given due diligence. The code cannot be unecessarily lengthy as this will require a lot of processing power as well as time. Larger codes could also cause small errors to generate bigger problems further down the line. The code should also be highly mobile and re-usable, this means that the code must be transferrable to other devices and not be limited to one single source.
Time constraints must also be considered. The use of pre-made elements or a Visual Programming language could help increase efficiency on a restricted time-schedule. A project with a longer time-frame will no doubt have much less errors generated, as this allows for more testing to be conducted.

1.7 - Describe legal guidelines and constraints that impact digital projects – How does GDPR impact digital projects?

GDPR stands for General Data Protection Regulation. It is the EU law for data prtection, data processing and data storage, designed to empower the individual. Personal data and information is defined as any information that can be used to identify an individual, whether this be name, age, gender or ethnic background.
Companies now must prove your consent to keep or use your information. Potential penalties are up to 4% of a companies annual turnover.
The new regulations have 7 main points.
Lawfulness and transparency. The processing of data must be lawful and transparent to the data subject.
Purpose. The company must process the data for a legitimate purpose, specified explicitly to the data subject when the data is collected.
Data minimization. The company must collect and process only as much data as absolutely necessary for the specified purpose.
Storage. The company may only store personally identifying data for as long as the purpose specified.
Accuracy. The company must keep personal data accurate and up to date.
Security and confidentiality. The processing must be done in a way so as to ensure appropriate security and confidentiality.
Accountability. The comapny and data controller will be responsible for being able to demonstrate GDPR compliance.
